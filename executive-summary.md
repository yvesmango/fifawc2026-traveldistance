# Executive Summary

This analysis answers a simple question: which national team had to travel the most by air to reach its 2026 World Cup group-stage matches? The short answer is South Africa, and the wider pattern is just as telling. The top of the table is dominated by long-haul travel and repeated venue hops, while the bottom of the table shows how much easier the tournament becomes when training sites and match venues sit closer together.

**Note:** the distance ranking shown here is a one-way sum from each team&apos;s training site to each group-stage venue. The dashboard&apos;s flight-time equivalent doubles that total and divides by 900 km/h to approximate round-trip time in the air.

## Top 5

1. South Africa - 44,614 km
2. Congo DR - 28,323 km
3. Haiti - 25,226 km
4. Iraq - 23,829 km
5. Germany - 18,052 km

South Africa is the clear outlier. The top four most-traveled teams all clear 23,000 km, which means the travel burden is large enough to shape recovery windows, logistics, and planning. Germany rounds out the top five, but there is still a noticeable gap between its total and the teams above it.

## Bottom 5

44. Ivory Coast - 541 km
45. Paraguay - 544 km
46. Ghana - 1,113 km
47. Egypt - 1,167 km
48. Argentina - 1,484 km

The bottom five teams face a far lighter travel load. The bottom two are separated by only 3 km, which shows just how compressed the low end of the ranking is. Compared with South Africa, the least-traveled team covers more than 80 times less distance.

## So What?

The ranking shows that geography still matters. Teams with training bases and venues clustered nearby gain a real logistical advantage, while others absorb the cost of long flight legs across a continent. Kilometers remain the canonical unit for the analysis; miles are shown only as a secondary reference in the dashboard.
