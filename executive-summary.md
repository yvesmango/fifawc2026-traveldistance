# Executive Summary

This analysis answers a simple question: which national team had to travel the most by air to reach its 2026 World Cup group-stage matches? The short answer is Norway, and the wider pattern is just as telling. The top of the table is dominated by long-haul travel and repeated venue hops, while the bottom of the table shows how much easier the tournament becomes when training sites and match venues sit closer together. South Africa now sits 42nd, which is a useful reality check that the one-way fallback fix is working.

**Note:** the distance ranking shown here is a one-way sum from each team&apos;s training site to each group-stage venue. The dashboard&apos;s flight-time equivalent uses that same one-way distance total and converts it at 900 km/h to give an in-air time estimate.

## Top 5

1. Norway - 31,696 km
2. Haiti - 25,226 km
3. Germany - 25,035 km
4. Iraq - 23,829 km
5. Jordan - 18,884 km

Norway is the clear outlier. The top four most-traveled teams all clear 23,000 km, which means the travel burden is large enough to shape recovery windows, logistics, and planning. Norway&apos;s one-way distance total translates to roughly 35 hours and 13 minutes in the air, while the rest of the top five still sit in the 21-to-28 hour range. That is a meaningful amount of travel time before a single ball is kicked.

  <li>Egypt - 1,167 km</li>

The bottom five teams face a far lighter travel load. The bottom two are separated by only 3 km, which shows just how compressed the low end of the ranking is. Compared with Norway, the least-traveled team covers nearly 60 times less distance.

The time-equivalent lens tells the same story from the other end of the table. Ivory Coast sits at roughly 36 minutes of in-air time, while Ghana, Egypt, Argentina, and Tunisia all stay under two hours. That contrast underscores how much geography changes the tournament experience for each squad.

## So What?

The ranking shows that geography still matters. Teams with training bases and venues clustered nearby gain a real logistical advantage, while others absorb the cost of long flight legs across a continent. Kilometers remain the canonical unit for the analysis; miles are shown only as a secondary reference in the dashboard. South Africa&apos;s 42nd-place finish is a good reminder that the earlier fallback bug had been inflating its total dramatically.
